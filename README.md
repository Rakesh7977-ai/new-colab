fundamental 

INDIAN STOCK FUNDAMENTALS

Fundamental analysis means studying the real business behind a stock.

A strong company usually has:
- steady revenue growth
- steady profit growth
- good margins
- good cash flow
- manageable debt
- strong return ratios
- clear business model
- honest and capable management
- demand that can last for many years

Important metrics:

Revenue growth:
Shows whether the company is increasing sales. Consistent revenue growth is usually a positive sign.

Profit growth:
Shows whether the company is becoming more profitable. Profit growth is stronger when it comes with good cash flow and stable margins.

EPS:
Earnings per share shows how much profit belongs to each share.

P/E ratio:
Price compared to earnings. Very high P/E can mean expensive valuation if growth does not support it.

P/B ratio:
Price compared to book value. Useful for banks, financials, and asset-heavy companies.

ROE:
Return on equity. Higher ROE usually means the company uses shareholder money efficiently.

ROCE:
Return on capital employed. This shows how efficiently the company uses all capital.

Debt-to-equity:
Higher debt means higher financial risk. Too much debt can become dangerous if profits fall.

Operating margin:
Shows how much profit remains from operations after direct costs.

Net profit margin:
Shows the final profit after all expenses, interest, and tax.

Cash flow:
A company may show profit on paper but still have weak cash flow. Real cash generation matters.

Promoter holding:
Promoter holding shows how much the founders or controlling owners own. Stable holding can be good, but pledged shares are a warning sign.

Institutional holding:
Mutual funds, foreign institutions, and insurance firms can signal confidence, but they can also exit quickly.

What is a good company:
- clear business
- strong demand
- repeat customers
- pricing power
- low debt
- good margins
- strong cash generation
- trustworthy management
- sustainable advantage or moat

Red flags:
- falling sales
- falling profit
- weak cash flow
- high debt
- poor governance
- frequent equity dilution
- sudden profit jumps without support
- weak return ratios
- inconsistent results

When judging valuation:
- a great business can still be a bad stock if too expensive
- a cheap stock is not always a good stock if the business is weak
- growth, quality, and valuation should be checked together









technical 

TECHNICAL ANALYSIS

Technical analysis studies price and volume.

The main idea:
Price reflects market sentiment. Volume shows the strength behind price movement.

Trend:
- Uptrend means price is making higher highs and higher lows
- Downtrend means price is making lower highs and lower lows
- Sideways means price is moving in a range

Support:
A price area where buying interest may appear.

Resistance:
A price area where selling pressure may appear.

Breakout:
When price moves above resistance with strong volume.

Breakdown:
When price falls below support with strong volume.

Volume:
High volume during an upward move is usually positive.
Low volume breakouts are weaker.

Moving averages:
- Price above moving averages can show strength
- Price below moving averages can show weakness
- Crossovers can give trend clues

RSI:
- Above 70 can mean overbought
- Below 30 can mean oversold
- RSI alone should not be used as the only signal

MACD:
Used to study momentum and possible trend changes.

Candlestick ideas:
- Doji may show indecision
- Hammer may show possible reversal
- Bullish engulfing may show buying strength
- Bearish engulfing may show selling pressure

Gap up:
Opening higher than previous close can show strong sentiment.

Gap down:
Opening lower than previous close can show negative sentiment.

Technical decision logic:
- Trend + volume + breakout = stronger setup
- Weak trend + low volume = avoid or wait
- Support break = caution
- Resistance break = confirmation needed

Important rule:
Do not use only one indicator.
Combine trend, support/resistance, volume, and momentum together.





risk and rules

RISK RULES FOR STOCK ANALYSIS

A stock becomes risky when:
- debt is too high
- profits are unstable
- management is uncertain
- cash flow is weak
- valuation is too expensive
- technical trend is weak
- bad news is increasing
- sector is under pressure

Risk levels:
Low risk:
- strong business
- good fundamentals
- stable sector
- strong trend
- positive news

Medium risk:
- some strength, but not enough confirmation
- mixed fundamentals
- unclear trend
- mixed news

High risk:
- poor fundamentals
- bad technical trend
- high debt
- negative news
- weak sector
- uncertain business model

Decision rules:
Buy:
- fundamentals strong
- technical trend strong
- news positive
- valuation acceptable

Hold:
- data mixed
- trend unclear
- waiting for confirmation

Sell or avoid:
- weak business
- weak trend
- strong negative news
- high debt
- poor cash generation

Wait:
- too much uncertainty
- incomplete data
- no clear edge
- not enough confirmation







sector notes



SECTOR NOTES FOR INDIAN STOCKS

IT sector:
- depends on US demand, global tech spending, margins, deal wins, currency movement
- strong dollar can sometimes help exporters
- weak global demand can hurt growth

Banking sector:
- depends on RBI policy, loan growth, deposit growth, credit quality, interest rates
- high credit growth is good if asset quality stays healthy
- bad loans and rising provisions are warning signs

Auto sector:
- depends on consumer demand, fuel prices, festival demand, interest rates, EV transition
- two-wheelers, passenger vehicles, and commercial vehicles move differently

Pharma sector:
- depends on exports, USFDA approvals, competition, pricing pressure, compliance
- regulatory problems can affect stock sharply

FMCG sector:
- usually defensive and stable
- slow but steady growth
- good during uncertain markets

Infrastructure sector:
- depends on government spending, orders, execution, debt, and working capital
- order book strength is important

Oil and energy sector:
- depends on crude prices, refining margins, global demand, policy, and supply shocks

Telecom sector:
- depends on ARPU, subscriber growth, pricing, capex, and competition

Retail and consumption:
- depends on consumer spending, expansion, margins, and brand strength

Metal sector:
- cyclical
- depends on global commodity prices, demand, input costs, and export trends

How to write news entries:
Date | Company | Type | Event | Possible effect | Source summary

Example format:

2026-04-12 | TCS | Sector news | Global IT spending remains cautious | Mixed for IT stocks | Deal wins and margins need monitoring

2026-04-12 | HDFC Bank | Banking news | Credit growth remains strong | Positive if asset quality is stable | Watch deposit growth and net interest margin

2026-04-12 | Infosys | Company news | Large deal pipeline and export demand remain important | Positive if demand stays healthy | Monitor US demand and margins

2026-04-12 | Auto sector | Sector news | Demand depends on interest rates and festival season | Mixed to positive | Watch margin pressure and sales volumes

2026-04-12 | Pharma sector | Sector news | Regulatory updates may affect specific companies | High impact if compliance issue appears | Watch USFDA and export orders

2026-04-12 | Market | Macro news | RBI policy or inflation news can change market mood | Affects broad market sentiment | Higher rates can pressure valuations

News categories to remember:
- company results
- expansion plans
- mergers and acquisitions
- management changes
- regulatory action
- legal issues
- sector growth
- market-wide risk
- RBI policy
- inflation
- global market movement
- crude oil movement
- foreign investor flows

How to judge news:
Positive news:
- better results
- new orders
- margin improvement
- debt reduction
- expansion
- guidance upgrade

Negative news:
- profit decline
- weak guidance
- fraud allegation
- regulatory issue
- debt stress
- margin pressure
- demand slowdown

Use news with caution:
- one good news item does not fix bad fundamentals
- one bad news item does not always destroy a good company
- judge the full picture

answer style 

ANSWER STYLE FOR THE AI

Always answer in this order:
1. Company summary
2. Fundamental view
3. Technical view
4. News view
5. Risk view
6. Final conclusion

Style rules:
- be clear and simple
- do not guess blindly
- mention uncertainty when data is weak
- separate fact from interpretation
- do not use only one signal
- if data is mixed, say WAIT or HOLD
- if the company is too weak, say AVOID
- if the setup is strong, say BUY only with caution

Always mention:
- what the company does
- whether business quality is strong or weak
- whether trend is strong or weak
- whether news is positive or negative
- whether risk is low, medium, or high
